01 — The Problem

A market gap between affordability and quality

In the early 2010s, Phnom Penh’s education market was rapidly evolving. A growing middle class of Cambodian families wanted their children to receive global education delivered in English while also maintaining Khmer language learning aligned with Cambodia’s Ministry of Education, Youth and Sport (MOEYS).

The market was polarized. Most international schools offered English-based programs at premium price points, largely serving high-income and expatriate families. Local schools following the MOEYS curriculum were more affordable but often lacked strong English immersion or globally aligned academic frameworks.

“Middle-income families were caught between two worlds: international schools they couldn’t afford, and local schools that couldn’t give their children global preparation. Bamboo was built to close that gap.”

This created a clear opportunity: an affordable hybrid school that combined international academic standards with the Khmer national curriculum — accessible, balanced, and purpose-built for families who were ambitious for their children but underserved by the existing market.

02 — Product Strategy

A hybrid curriculum designed for two worlds

The school was designed as a hybrid education model integrating international and local academic frameworks — giving families genuine bilingual education without forcing them to choose between global preparation and national identity.

Early Childhood

Montessori (NAMC)

North American Montessori Center methodology — hands-on learning, child independence, and self-directed exploration.

Elementary

Pearson Global Schools

English Language Arts and Mathematics aligned with Common Core standards for a rigorous, internationally recognized foundation.

All Levels

MOEYS Khmer Curriculum

Cambodia’s national curriculum integrated across all grades, building strong Khmer language foundations.

This hybrid structure let Bamboo serve families who wanted international quality without sacrificing cultural and linguistic grounding — while staying compliant with national education requirements, a real operational and trust advantage. Enrichment in coding, music, and visual arts extended the curriculum beyond core academics for students aged 2.5–10 years.

03 — Learning Environment

Built from the ground up for the methodology

Rather than adapting a standard building into classrooms, I designed a purpose-built Montessori learning environment — where every spatial and material decision served how children actually learn. The school occupies a three-floor corner building in BKK1, Chamkarmon.

800m²

Three-floor building

On 560 m² of land in central Phnom Penh.

9

Classrooms

25 students max each — 190 total capacity, sized 55–65 m².

Each classroom was designed with specific features supporting the Montessori approach and child safety:

Custom Montessori bookshelves accessible to children
Child-friendly furniture and learning materials
Non-slip laminate flooring for safety
A dedicated restroom in each classroom for independence

Every classroom ran on a consistent staffing model — one lead teacher, one assistant teacher, and two nannies — ensuring students received both academic instruction and attentive daily care.

04 — Campus Experience

Designing for the whole child

The campus was designed to support both academic learning and physical development. Learning doesn’t stop at the classroom door — the full environment shapes how children feel, move, and grow throughout the day.

Playground for physical play and motor development
Sandbox for sensory and creative exploration
Garden space for nature-based learning
Small swimming pool
Reading area at student pickup
ICT computer room for digital literacy

Enrichment programming — coding, music, and visual arts — was embedded into the weekly schedule, so students developed creativity and digital skills alongside core academics.

05 — Digital Systems

Closing the loop between school and home

Parent trust is a school’s most important asset. I implemented digital tools that gave parents real visibility into their child’s learning — transforming family engagement from passive to active.

Progress Tracking

MontessoriCompass

Tracked student learning progress based on Montessori activities and teacher observations — a structured window into each child’s development.

Communication

ClassDojo

Real-time communication between teachers and parents — daily updates, photos, and direct messaging that deepened engagement.

Together, these systems reduced the anxiety parents feel when they can’t observe the classroom — especially important for families new to Montessori who needed to build confidence in the approach over time.

07 — Lessons Learned

What building a school taught me about building products

1

The product is the experience, not the offering. Parents don’t enroll in a curriculum — they enroll in a feeling of safety, progress, and belonging. Every touchpoint from the first inquiry call to the daily ClassDojo update is the product.

2

Hybrid models are powerful but operationally demanding. Integrating three curriculum frameworks created a genuinely differentiated product — but meant every staffing, scheduling, and assessment decision had to work across all three. Simplicity deserves constant defense.

3

Build for transfer from day one. The exit only worked because the school could run without me. That required years of documentation, team development, and resisting the temptation to be the single point of knowledge.

4

Location is product strategy. Choosing BKK1 was a positioning decision — central, accessible to commuting parents, surrounded by the demographic we were designing for. Where you sit in a city shapes who finds and trusts you.

5

Parent trust compounds slowly and breaks quickly. Reputation is the growth engine in education. The investment in MontessoriCompass and ClassDojo paid returns far beyond the tools — visibility builds trust; trust drives retention and referral.
